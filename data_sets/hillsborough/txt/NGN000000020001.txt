Dear Sir,

Re:   THE SUN - Wednesday, April 19th, 1989

I am writing to register a complaint against the above newspaper, specifically
the use of a photograph of Lee Nidhol lying fatally injured on Page 3 of that
edition.

I find the use of such a picture offensive personally, and can only imagine the
distress the picture will cause to his family, relatives and school friends. I
have no desire to censor the media, and think that in general they responsibly
performed their duty to inform people of this tragedy. My concern is more at
the timing of the picture, which at this date I consider to have been used for
sensationalism only. I have spoken to a journalist at The Sun, but do not
accept as valid his comments that the boy's death the previous day made the use
of the picture justifiable.

May I add that at 41 years old, I have never before seen fit to contact a
newspaper or The Press Council, and that not only do I consider myself to be
broad-minded but, having lived in Northern Ireland during the height of the
troubles, also to be fairly hardened to the sight of death.

Finally I would add that as I am not a football supporter, my complaint is not
influenced by the front page of the same newspaper edition, the truths or
otherwise of which will no doubt be established by the official enquiry.

Yours faithfully,

                                         r8:                    ) Front Page ~ Monday’ 17th Apri1’ 1989•

                           I am writing to you to register my utter disgust in the decision
                           of these two newspapers to publish photographs of what were clearly
                           dead and dying people in the tragic accident at the Hillsborough
                           Football Ground on Saturday, 15th April.

                           In the Daily Mirror this picture was in full colour occupying
                           the front and back pages; in The Sun it was on the front page.
                           This struck me as being the most appalling and tasteless example
                           of photo reporting I have ever seen, and I cannot imagine the
                           added stress and distress that this must cause the relatives and
                           friends of those who have died or been injured.

                           Since it would seem to me that the individual newspapers have
                           lost all sense of propriety, decency and suitable moral standards,
                           I feel that the Press Council should insist on a clear definition
                           of what is acceptable and what is not acceptable in the standard
                           of reporting, particularly on tragedies like these.

                           The photographs of dead people in such close-up detail has, to
                           my way of thinking, put back standards even further, and the
                           industry does owe the public a greater responsibility than clearly
                           these two newspapers are practising.

                           I would not expect to be alone in being sickened by this most
                           recent example, and I hope the Council will agree that this
                           represents an unforgivable and unrepeatable transgression.

 To: The Press Council,




 18th April, 1989


 Dear Sirs,

      I am writing to express my disgust at the photographs published in
 yesterday's Daily Mirror and Sun respectively.

     The two-page colour photograph in the Daily Mirror was insensitive to say
the least, and I fail to see how any person in their right mind could possibly
justify publishing a photograph of dead and dying young people. I just thank
God that I am not the mother of one of those young people, but I am a
Liverpool mother and on behalf of Liverpool mothers I deplore how this type of
gutter journalism is allowed to exist.

     I have never written a letter like this in my life, I do not normally
complain about what is printed in our papers, but on this occasion I refuse to
be silent.

     I am sincerely asking the Press Council to do whatever is in their power
to reprimand publicly the Daily Mirror,      There are many families suffering
deeply in Liverpool today, their grief, their horror of having lost loved ones
in the tragedy of the Hillsborough Stadium disaster is bad enough. But to
have to see that photograph spread all over the paper, I am sure it will have
affected them for the rest of their lives.

     Please God the dead of Hillsborough will rest in peace - the living will
find it much more difficult!


                              Yours faithfully,




p.s. a signed petition is enclosed.

 The Press Council,
 1 Salisbury Square,

 Dear Sirs,

 After a week of careful deliberation, I feel I must write to complain
 bitterly of the behaviour of much of the British Press in connection
 with the Hillsborough Tragedy.

 I confess, each morning I have watched the stories unfold over the
 shoulders of other commuters reading the articles following last
 Saturdays events and I feel the Press are almost wholely responsible
 for the wrangling, di^ntion, outrage and misery that has been caused.

Each day, a newspaper picked up another line and inflammed it out
of all proportion: one day, The Sun shouts at us that all Liverpool
fans are disgusting hooligans, the next the Mirror tells us that all
police who were present on that awful day are callous, uncaring bastards.

I have not seen one newspaper mention the fact that, whatever individual
fans or members of the police have done, there were thousands upon
thousands of people in that stadium behaving fabulously well and in
some cases, saving people's lives.   Such news as this has been committed
to the last paragraph in small print on any article.

The other issue which has resulted is the printing of those terrible
photographs of people dying at Hillsborough.   No-one would suggest
that such things should be whitewashed and prettied up for the sake
of the public - but I'm sure everyone can well imagine what its like
to be crushed to death.   Do we really need all that pictorial evidence?
Far more to the point, what of relatives and friends picking up a newspaper
and seeing the people they love dying all over the front of it’   Do
c ^ yp^oJide?need 3 grea^ memento of what they suffered than their imagination

1 W°UidJ enture to suggest that without the efforts of the British Press
a good three quarters of the recriminations, arguments and (probably
incurable) chasms that have been set between the Police and Football
Fans (and also probably many ordinary people in Liverpool and Sheffield)
would never have happerf^   Also, that many families faced far more
distress because of their reporting of the deaths.
