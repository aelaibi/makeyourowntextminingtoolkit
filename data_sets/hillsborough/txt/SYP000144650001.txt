         I feel that I have to write to you today after
reading reports in my newspaper of remarks made hy yourself

distance from "both Liverpool and Yorkshire and certainly have
no connection with either area, hut I am a 'human' human "being

hy what has happened, yet without any thought for them you
rush to the defence of your police force.     I have the
utmost respect for the police and believe that the tragedy
was caused, as usually happens, hy several factors leading
up to a climax.

Inquiry and not to reporters.   I do not doubt that    you know
many fine dedicated men in your Police Force - in a    rush
to defend them you overlooked the effect your words    would
have on families already numb with sorrow.
         As a representative of the people you are not
allowed such mistakes. . . . 1 have always voted Conservative
and feel that you have let them down.

Dear Mr.Moynihan.

Proposed Football Membership Scheme

"Don't confuse me with the facts - my mind is made up". A wise man said
this about somebody a long time ago but it appears to be just as true today.

If it is correctly reported on the BBC lunchtime news today that your
leader, Mrs.Thatcher is still determined to go ahead with the Football
Membership Scheme, then I for one must have serious doubts about voting
for a party prepared to tolerate such an arrogant, know all leader.
Hitherto, I have had little sympathy with those who attack her but
this matter of the Football Membership Scheme gives one an insight as to
what they are getting at, and if correctly reported, you appear to be
cast in the same mould.  My concern goes beyond the question of the
proposed scheme. What worries me is the faulty judgement of those who
are sure that they are right, to the extent of refusing to postpone - at
the very least - the proposed scheme until the results of the enquiry are
known.  How far does this intolerant attitude of mind extend to other areas
of public life ?

As I said in my letter of 4th April, the root cause of the trouble lies
not with football but with the lack of discipline in modern society in
our Country and the events at Hillsborough bear this out, however unpopular
it may be to express such views. Only today there are reports in the
West Sussex Gazette - our local weekly paper - that steps are being taken
in an attempt to reduce the hooliganism at Goodwood Races this summer.  No
doubt somebody will blame football for the trouble.  Oh that there were more
politicians like Mr.Patnick prepared to speak out about the real cause of
the trouble in our society, rather than tinkering with dotty membership
schemes.

Yours sincerely,

Dear Mr. Patnick,


I felt I had to respond to the comments attributed to you in the
Daily Telegraph dated April 19th, in respect of the terrible
tragedy at Hillsborough Football Ground.


May I state at the outset that I am an Area Director of a major
Bank and that myself and my wife have been watching football for
over thirty years.    My purpose in stating this, is to firs
all make it quite clear that I am a fairly responsible citizen
who has had a wide experience in watching football in various
parts of the UK, which perhaps qualifies me to speak fair y
authoritatively on what I saw last Saturday at Hillsborough.


 I was seated in the North Stand on Saturday afternoon and some
 twenty minutes before the kick-off, I commented to my twenty-six
 year old son that I could not understand why the SJJPP?^®**
 the Leppings Lane entrance had all been coraled into
 two pens, whilst the two end pens remained over half empty. Thi
 clearly indicated that neither the Police nor the officials at
 the Club were monitoring the number of people
 and that the tickets sold had not been identified according
 any particular pen number.


 Regrettably, this observation was to prove all too ominous       as
 this dreadful afternoon unfolded in front of our eyes.
The crowd outside the ground had been in a boisterous but good
humoured mood and whereas, undoubtedly one or two fans had had a
drink or two, there was certainly no-one falling around drunk
and disorderly and the horrendous build up outside the Leppings
Lane entrance was due entirely to the Police failing to marshal
and intercept the crowd two or three hundred yards from the
entrance which they clearly should have done, bearing in mind
the very narrow entrance and the minimum number of turnstiles
available.


What I witnessed after the incident was a tremendous all round
effort by the fans to save their colleagues, many of them
running numerous times up and down the ground with makeshift
stretchers carrying bodies of the dead and dying.     Others were
giving heart and chest massage and mouth to mouth resuscitation
and at no stage did I see anything that remotely resembled
Police being attacked or hindered in any shape or form.


I would go so far to say that had it not been for the prompt
reaction of the spectators, the tragedy would have been far
worse, as it was them and not the Police who helped to pass out
and protect those people in the front of the pen who were being
badly crushed.   Furthermore, it was the fans who tore down the
advertising boards on the perimeter of the ground when it became
quite obvious that the emergency services and the efforts of the
Police were totally inadequate.


Furthermore, when Police reinforcements did arrive, some twenty
minutes after the incident, they stood in front of the North
Stand for at least ten minutes, apparently fearing an invasion
from the occupants of the seats in that stand.       This clearly
indicated the inability of the officers in control to grasp the
seriousness of the situation despite the fact that, by then it
was obvious that people had been killed in significant numbers.


It is quite clear from your remarks that you were not yourself
present at the match and you have been over-influenced by the
Police who are now anxious to save their own skins in the
comments that you have made.        I have to say that as a
Conservative voter I regard your remarks as quite abhorrent and
insensitive, particularly to the families of the bereaved most
of whom were still awaiting funerals when you felt it necessary
to speak out in your ill-informed manner.
I feel you owe an apology to the people         of   Liverpool   and
particularly to the families of the bereaved.


Having   attended  many  major   football  occasions  during  my
lifetime, may I suggest that rather than attempting to apportion
the blame on the spectators, you ask yourself and your friends
in the Police force the following questions:-


1)    Why was it that the crowd was allowed to build up outside
      the Leppings Lane entrance prior to the kick-off, when
      quite clearly crowd control should have been carried out
      well away from the ground, particularly bearing in mind
      the narrow entrance and lack of turnstiles.


2)    Why were the vast majority of spectators coraled into the
      two centre pens well before the kick-off, when it was
      quite evident that the side pens were virtually empty.


3)    If it was necessary to open the gate at the Leppings Road
      end (which must surely to God remain debatable) , then why
      were the supporters not channelled into the two empty
      pens rather than allowed to charge straight into the pens
      that were already full. It was obvious to everyone else
      on the ground that this would result in a major tragedy
      and the officers in charge should surely have been aware
      of this.


4)    Why after the crush had occurred-were the Police so
      slow and loathe to react to what was, quite clearly a
      major tradegy and why did they remain obsessed with the
      possibility   of   a   ground   invasion    from obviously
      respectable people sitting in stand seats, whilst at the
      other side of the ground people were dying.


May I also suggest that you listen to the comments of the two
Doctors who were hauled out of the crowd to attend the dead and
dying rather than the biased and unjust comments coming from
what, I can only believe to be, a minority of some of the
Sheffield Police force.
I am afraid that the responsibility for this disaster lies quite
clearly with the Senior Officer in charge of crowd control and
organisation.   The Police should have been well aware that a
large part of the crowd would arrive        fairly late and in
boisterous high spirits having had a few to drink.       This is
nothing new, it has been going on for years - what did they
expect a crowd of choir boys?


I do hope Mr. Patnick that you will take the trouble to reply to
this letter in some detail, as I and many others who were in
attendance at Hillsborough are anxious that the record should be
put straight.

    Dear Irvine Patnick,

                               HTUKBOFOJffl DISASTER

                            POLICING OUTSIDE LEFPINGS LANE
                              AT APPROXIMATELY 2.45 P.M.


    I am aged 48 years, Senior Partner of this firm and went to the Foctball
    Match with my two friends from Liverpool, one of whom is retired and one of
    whom is a BA 747 Pilot.

    We planned to arrive at Gate "A” of Leppings Lane entrance at. 2.30 p.E.   to
    meet a friend there.   We had a ticket for him. We had £14.00 ickets for the
    North Stand.    We approached the Ground from Penistone Road ha\ ing parked a
    long distance away.     When we got to the Penistone Road side of the Ground
    just before 2.30p.m. we were told that we would have to walk right round the
    Ground to gain entrance at the Leppings Lane end.    However we were not able
    to go along any of the small roads from Penistone Road to Leppings Lane i.e.
    Vere Road, Fielding Road or Bickerton Road but were told we had to go right
    to the roundabout at Herries Road where we should turn left.    This we did.
    This delayed us. I did not expect to have to make such a detour.

     I would estimate we arrived outside the Leppings Lane entrance at about 2.40
     p.m.   We would have been there earlier had we not had to make such a long
     detour.

     When we got there, there were many people in a great mass outside the Gates.
     There seemed to be no queues for the entrances.

     We were unable to see out friend in the crowd.    Because we were already in
     the crush moving towards the three Gates "A we were unable to stop and wait
     for him but were pushed forward towards the Gates.

     So far as I can recall there were a number of Policemen outside the big blue
     Gates, some of whom were on horseback.    They seemed to be doing nothing at
     all to control the crowds.

     I  saw no ill humour or drunkeness of any sort outside the Ground.   There
          no anger until I was pushed right up against the brick cabin holding
     the turnstiles.      Because we were all being crushed from behind we were


     getting frightened and angry. People were shouting back over their shoulders
     asking those behind not to push.                                        ,
     People were shouting at the Police Officers by the side of the turnstiles
     demanding that they ask their superiors to take action.
I would estimate that I was in the crush** outside the three Gates      marked
"A" for the North Stand between about 2.40 p.m. and 2.55 p.m.

I was eventually crushed up against the brickwall of the turnstile.     By
this time I was separated from my companions, both of whom are about 6ft
tall.    Immediately in front of me at one time was a woman in a white
annorak.    She was being crushed against the wall and became hysterical.
She could not move towards the turnstile because of the weight of numbers.
Somehow or other those of us around her managed to push back from the wall
sufficient for her to get through the turnstile.

At this time there was a young male Police Officer and and young woman
Police Officer immediately in front of me.    I had been pushed over towards
the right, away from the three Gates marked "A".      I think that the woman
Police Officer was manning the Gate at the right.       As I got near to them
the crowd around me demanded that the male Police Officer do something and
he tried to climb the brickwall of the Cabin housing the turnstile.    He was
not able to do this but I believe eventually got through the turnstile to
the inside of the ground.     The woman Police Officer remained in front of
me.    She became more and more alarmed as she was being crushed against the
brickwall.    She did not seem to say anything at all but looked completely
terrified. I think she got into the        ground just before me.

I eventually got    tothe righthand of the turnstiles marked "A” when      the
crowd swayed again tothe left.       The person immediately in front of      me
seemed to have only half a ticket and the person on the        turnstilesaid
something to him, to which he replied that he had lost the other half in the
crowd approaching the turnst i]e and could not. bend down to pick it up.     He.
ras allowed entrance to the ground.           I followed him in through    the
turnstile and would estimate that I was on the inside of Gate "A" at
approximately 2.55 p.m. because I just got to my seat on row "H” of the of
the North Stand just as the game started..

Having got through the turnstile, along with many other people I immediately
turned to my left where there were a mrnber of Police Officers - I would
estimate between 10 and 20 standing about .    People were screaming at them
and shouting with great anger to take control of the situation outside the
gates.    A black Police Officer shouted something back to me, I am not sure
what he said.

Many people walking from the turnstile to their seats were angry      and
incredulous at the scenes that we had just been through outside the gate.
When I arrived at my seat my 51 year old male friend was white faced.  He


 said he had been supporting Liverpool for a very long time and      had   never
 seen such ineffective crowd control outside a ground.

 I was very shaken.

                      AFTER THE INCIDENT INSIDE THE GROUND

 Very soon after the incident a man in the row behind showed me the whole of
 his ticket.   He had not had to part with any part of this in gaining
 entrance to the ground.     I remember other people nearby saying that they
had not been asked for their ticket.

From my seat I had a clear view of the Leppings Lane enclosure behind the
goal.    Along with many other people it took me a long time to realise that
something serious had occurred when the game was stopped.      I think that
there w4L& very sad failures to deal with the situation.          Among the
failures that occurred after the game was stopped, I would list the
following

1.     For a very long time no medical help was given to people     who     were
       obviously injured and lying on the pitch.

2.     For a very long time, most of the Police on the pitch stood about
       doing nothing, even when spectators were carrying their injured and
       dead friends on advertising hoardings towards the far end of the
       ground. Sometimes the Police and the spectators helped to carry those
       that were injured or dead.

3.      It seemed   a very very long time before any Ambulances       arrived
       anywhere near the Leppings Lane end of the ground.

4.     The tannoy loudspeaker system was hardly used at all either to direct
       the crowd or to inform the crowd.   I heard no demand for a Doctor or
       other medical services up until the time I left at about 3.55 p.m. I
       heard only one request early on for the spectators to       clear the
       pitch.

      I heard one request that spectators clear the Leppings Lane enclosure
      which also was a long time after the incident.

5.     No use was made of anyone in authority including the two Managers to
      give either  encouragement, information or direction.        This was
      particularly unfortunate as for a long time much of the crowd were
      unaware of the seriousness of what had occurred and there were
      occasional dangers of confrontation, particularly when one Liverpool
      fan ran towards the Nottingham Forrest end of the ground.

6.      No use   was made of the Electronic Notice Board up untilthe time    I


      left the ground.       People around me were particularly angered when
      it became clear that people who were dead were being carried past us
      and the Electronic Notice Board continued to give out advertisements.

On walking away from the ground with my friends I saw many people who       were
very distressed and extremely angry with the Police.      I saw many        many
people shouting at the Police.

Whilst   I have no knowledge of the immediate cause of the crush in      the
Leppings Lane enclosure that caused people to die, I am in no doubt that the
lack of effective crowdcontrol outside the Leppings Lane entrance between
approximately 2.30 p.m. and 3.p.m. caused a very serious hazard and risk to
life.

I hope you can acknowledge receipt of this letter.
