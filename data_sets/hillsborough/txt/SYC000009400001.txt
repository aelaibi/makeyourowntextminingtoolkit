             Dear Sirs,

             Re: RESUMED INQUESTS 19th NOVEMBER 1990.


             If you are not already aware, I intend to resume the Inquests into the deaths
             of the 95 people who died at Hillsborough, one or more of whom you represent,
             on the 19th of November 1990 at 10.a.m. Details of venue will be notified in
             due course.

             I do not have to remind you of the fact that a considerable amount of
             evidence has already been taken, nor of the nature and limitations of a
             coronal investigation which you will recollect is not adversarial but
             inquisitorial in nature, and which of course has only limited objectives.

             Subject to the Coroners’ Act 1988 and the Coroners’ Rules, the Inquest is
             under my control, and in particular, it is my responsibility to select the
             ■witnesses who wish to give evidence. I am at present engaged in considering
             this and in determining who I wish to invite to attend.

             ■Without prejudice in any way to my rights in the matter,I am writing  to you
             to give you an opportunity to draw to my attention the names and other
             appropriate details of any witness or witnesses whom you would like me to
             consider calling to give evidence at the resumed Inquests.

             May I please ask you in considering whether you wish to draw any names to my
             attention, to bear in mind the scope and limitations of an Inquest.

             I also want to make it absolutely clear that the submission of a name or
             names for consideration is nothing more than that, and that thedecision  as
             to who to call to give evidence is and remains a matter for me.

             I would be grateful if you could respond as soon as possible. If I have not
             heard from you by the end of SepteniJ>er I will assume you have no names to
             submit.

             Yours faithfully,

CONVERSATION WITH MRS McBRIEN, FRIDAY, 8th MARCH 1991

At the conclusion of today's hearing I asked Mrs McBrien to come
and see me. Mr Mervyn Jones and Caroline Perkins remaining in
the room.


I explained to Mrs McBrien that I was getting very anxious about her
because I felt that things were now beginning to get on top of her.
She denied this. I also said that I was unhappy that I had to keep
on interrupting her with her questioning but although I tried to give
her ample liberty, the pressure of time was now such that I wou ld not
be able to let her ask questions which had already been dealt with
or go over ground which was already well covered. I therefore asked
her if she would be good enough to try to concentrate on what was
being said and to tailor her questions to the matters which had to be
dealt with in the inquest. I explained that I was not trying to
muzzle her by preventing her from asking questions, on the contrary,
I tried to give her considerable liberty, but it was necessary that
things were done properly and expeditiously. She responded by saying
that the witnesses were important and perhaps more important than
some of the local residents. I agreed that they were important
and repeated that I wasn't trying to stop her asking appropriate
questions but that in many instances the points she was raising
were not relevant to the inquest. She acknowledged that this was
so in fact and explained that many of the points which she was raising
were matters of importance to her and which she needed for her own
psychological wellbeing. I pointed out to her that whilst these
were important aspects, in the final analysis the inquest was dealing
with who, when, where and how.


She said that she was doing it for John and that losing him had not
only destroyed his life but also hers, and that she was doing what
she believed he would want her to do, namely, justice.

I then took the opportunity of saying that irrespective of what was
happening at the inquest and whatever the verdict might be, it should
